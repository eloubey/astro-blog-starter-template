Fired Fed. I am considering cutting off every Orange Man supporter.
Got axed. Not going to say which agency.

I’ve always considered myself extremely tolerant and willing to love people as they are… even if we don’t agree on everything.

I’ve never been an Orange Man supporter, but I’ve kept it civil with friends and family that were. Some of them liked having a civil conversation about him. Some were belligerent about politics, so we didn’t bring it up and tried to enjoy each other’s company.

Getting cut from a great job that I really believed in with no notice has been extremely traumatic. It’s still raw, but I feel so personally betrayed by those that voted for him. I can’t see past the politics anymore when I look at these people I care/cared about.

Some have been contrite and apologetic, but then turn around and support him and VP Musk on social media.

I just can’t right now.

I’m thinking about posting something and wishing the whole herd all of the best, hope they have a good life, but I won’t be in it. Or maybe I just quietly block all of them and focus on myself for a while.

I do know that I will need to talk to my in-laws. I’ve always had a great relationship with them, but thinking of going to their house and watching Fox News almost makes me sick.

AITAH for feeling this way? Does it make me an AH for cutting all these 20+ year relationships off? Am I overreacting and acting out of emotion?


🧾 Story Summary

The OP, a federal employee, was recently fired unexpectedly, causing deep emotional trauma. This experience has made them reassess relationships with friends and family who support former President Donald Trump (referred to as “Orange Man” in the post).

They have historically tried to maintain civil relationships despite political differences. Some friends and family enjoyed respectful debate, while others avoided political conversations entirely.

After the job loss, however, seeing these same people supporting Trump and his political allies online has triggered feelings of betrayal and anger. The OP is considering cutting off more than 20 longstanding relationships, including friends and possibly in-laws, at least temporarily.

They are torn between posting a public farewell or quietly removing these people from their life to focus on personal healing.

The OP asks: Am I the AH for feeling this way or cutting ties with longtime friends and family?

🧠 Behavior & Psychology Analysis
1. The OP

The OP’s reaction is a mix of grief, anger, and betrayal.

Trauma response: Losing a meaningful job without notice is a shock, and it’s natural to look for causes or symbols of the betrayal.
Political beliefs as a trigger: Their anger is intertwined with political identity and perceived complicity of others who voted for the administration.
Self-preservation: Considering cutting ties is an attempt to protect mental and emotional health.

Psychologically:

This is a stress-induced reevaluation of social bonds
Likely heightened emotional sensitivity due to job loss
Normal human reaction to feeling betrayed by those you care about
2. Friends & Family
Their continued support for Trump is within their rights.
Some have tried to apologize or explain themselves, indicating awareness of the OP’s feelings.
The OP’s anger may feel justified, but it’s based more on symbolic association than personal wrongdoing.

Psychologically:

The disconnect arises because shared values are deeply tied to trust and identity
People we care about making decisions we strongly disagree with can feel like personal betrayal, even if it’s not intentional
3. Emotional Context
OP is grieving a job, career, and possibly sense of stability
Politics becomes a lens to process anger
The desire to cut ties may be temporary—a way to regain control and emotional distance
⚖️ Verdict

NTA (Not The Asshole) for needing space, but context matters:

Self-care is valid. Protecting your mental health after trauma is important.
Cutting ties doesn’t make you an AH if it’s framed as a personal boundary, not punishment.
Communication matters: Letting people know it’s about your need for space—rather than attacking their beliefs—reduces relational fallout.

⚠️ Potential consequence: Some relationships may never fully recover, especially if this becomes permanent.

💬 Final Thoughts

This story highlights the intersection of personal trauma, politics, and social bonds.

Emotional responses to job loss are natural and valid.
Political disagreements can feel like betrayals when they touch identity and trust.
Taking space—quietly or publicly—is a healthy boundary when needed.
Over time, some relationships may heal; others may not.

The key is recognizing: you’re not overreacting morally—you’re responding to real emotional pain.
