Someone at work stole my chair. So I had them arrested.
I got a new job in sales working for a company that sells access control systems, CCTV systems, etc. The chair in my cubicle was uncomfortable AF, so I day 3 I brought in my own Herman Miller Aeron chair. On day 4 it was missing. I looked around and another sales rep was sitting in it. I asked for it back but he basically told me to F off.

I went and spoke to the owner of the company. He told me that the chairs aren't reserved. They are first come first serve. I explained that I OWN the chair and it cost me $1800. He told me that I need to come in earlier if I want that chair.

The next day the same guy was sitting in it when I got to the office. I told him to give it back or I was going to call the cops. He refused. So I called the cops.

It was quite the scene, but in the end he admitted that he took my EIGHTEEN HUNDRED DOLLAR chair. I was prepared. I had the receipt which showed the serial number. (Yes, Aeron chairs have serial numbers.) They asked me if I wanted to press charges. My new boss said I would be terminated if I had him arrested, so I said "Absolutely! I want to press charges!"

My new boss fired me on the spot, so I carried my chair out with me a I was leaving. And yes, the cops arrested the dude who took my chair (He admitted to it!).

The dude who took my chair has been in the industry for about 15 years, but he's going to lose his security license, which means he won't be able to work in the industry. He's going to lose his job. I don't give a shit. He shouldn't have stolen my chair.

Am I the asshole for making this dude lose his job?

🧾 Story Summary

A new sales employee brings their own Herman Miller Aeron chair (worth $1,800) to the office because the provided chair is uncomfortable. On the next day, another sales rep takes the chair and refuses to return it.

The employee reports it to the owner, who dismisses the concern, saying chairs are first-come, first-serve. The thief sits in the chair again the following day.

The employee calls the police, provides proof of ownership, and the coworker admits to stealing it. The employee presses charges, despite their boss warning that doing so would result in termination. They are fired, but the chair is retrieved, and the thief faces legal consequences, including potential loss of their security license.

The OP asks: Am I the AH for effectively causing this coworker to lose his job?

🧠 Behavior & Psychology Analysis
1. The Employee (OP)
Asserted property rights: The chair was privately owned, with clear proof of ownership (receipt and serial number).
Attempted internal resolution: Initially went to the owner before involving authorities.
Faced a threat: Employer warned that pressing charges would lead to termination.
Followed through legally: Stole was a criminal act, not a minor workplace dispute.

Psychologically:

OP acted out of justice and self-preservation
Maintained boundaries and personal property rights
Reaction shows low tolerance for theft and clear understanding of consequences
2. The Thief
Repeatedly took property that wasn’t theirs
Ignored requests for return
Demonstrated disregard for workplace ethics and property

Psychologically:

Habitual entitlement (15 years in industry without consequences?)
Poor impulse control and lack of professional integrity
3. Workplace Context
Owner’s response was inadequate, dismissing theft as a seating issue
Management’s reaction to pressing charges (threat of firing) shows fear of workplace conflict rather than ethical guidance
Workplace culture might tolerate unethical behavior, which contributed to escalation
⚖️ Verdict

NTA (Not The Asshole)

Why:

The chair was legally and rightfully theirs ✅
The coworker committed a criminal act, not just a minor rudeness or misunderstanding ✅
Seeking legal remedy is reasonable, especially after internal avenues failed ✅

Yes, the thief losing their job is a consequence—but it is a direct result of their own misconduct, not an overreaction by OP.

💬 Final Thoughts

This story highlights a few lessons:

Know your property rights: Documentation like receipts and serial numbers matter.
Persistence matters: OP tried internal resolution first but had to escalate.
Workplace ethics are crucial: Theft and repeated disregard for property have real consequences.
Legal consequences are fair when rules are broken: The employee didn’t create the fallout; they enforced accountability.

In short: sometimes, standing up for yourself has serious consequences for others—but that’s the point of accountability.
