AITAH for telling my husband that he absolutely ruined the birth of our child?
Hi everyone. Our daughter is now 8 weeks old, so obviously this whole argument has gone on a very very long time. We both have been holding grudges and neither of us think that we are wrong. My husband does not know I am posting this, so I am going to keep it as anonymous as possible.

So when I got pregnant with my daughter, my husband started in immediately telling me that I should have a home birth. I really do not know why he was so adamant on it, but he was. At first, I brushed him off and told him I would think about it because I was only 6 weeks pregnant, and the birth seemed so far off.

Of course, it came quickly, and my husband would literally speak over me at doctors' appointments when my doctor would ask if I had a birth plan.

This caused a few arguments between us in those 39 weeks of pregnancy, but I never really changed my mind. Eventually my husband's mother sat down and talked to me, and she told me all of the reasons why they did not want me to go to a hospital for the birth. I expressed my concerns about you know, safety of the baby and myself but just like my husband, she brushed me off.

I ended up telling my husband that I would take myself to the hospital when it was time and that I did not want a home birth. He acted as if he didn't hear me. We met with a doula who was also very pushy. I felt overwhelmed and not supported at all. I was 36 weeks at that point.

So, when I went into labor, I was 39 weeks, and I begged, absolutely begged my husband to take me to the hospital where my doctor is. He wouldn't. He spoke to me condescendingly and called the doula instead. I was in labor for about 3 days, active labor for around the last 22 hours.

I cried the whole time. I just felt something was wrong. I was scared and often times they left me alone. The doula told me that if active pushing and labor reached 24 hours, I had to go into the hospital. I remember thinking that I could not decide which was worse- staying in labor for another 2 hours or having my baby right there. When she was finally out, I don't even remember wanting to hold her. I just remember crying out of relief.

Obviously, I am okay now, but I did not have a good experience. On my first appointment after birth with my doctor, she was very shocked I had the baby. She was concerned. I was so upset.

I told my husband that he absolutely ruined it for me. I truly never want to go through that again. I hear mothers say that they forget all the pain the second they have the baby, but I didn't. I love my daughter so much, but it was horrible, and it was entirely his fault.

So, I told him that, several times. He rolls his eyes every time and tells me how mothers are "strong" and how I am not trying to be strong. I told him that if we ever have another baby - which he wants - that I will never do a home birth ever again. His response is "we'll see". I cannot possibly be TA here, can I? Everyone around me is acting like this is so normal, but it's not. Is it?



🧾 Story Summary

A woman shares her traumatic birth experience. During her pregnancy, her husband was insistent that she have a home birth. Despite her repeated objections, he dominated conversations at doctor’s appointments, spoke over her, and even involved his mother and a doula to push his preference.

When labor began at 39 weeks, she begged to go to the hospital, but he refused and called the doula instead. Labor lasted three days, with active labor for 22 hours, during which she often felt scared, unsupported, and alone. She remembers crying the entire time and only feeling relief once her daughter was finally born.

Afterward, she told her husband he ruined the birth for her and expressed that she would never agree to a home birth in the future. He dismissed her feelings, saying mothers are “strong” and implying she wasn’t trying hard enough, responding to her concerns with “we’ll see” about future children.

She’s now questioning: was she wrong to be so blunt about how this experience affected her?

🧠 Behavior & Psychology Analysis
1. The Wife (OP)

Her reaction is fully justified and rooted in trauma.

Autonomy violated: Her bodily autonomy and birth preferences were repeatedly ignored.
Emotional trauma: 3 days of labor, unsupported, and feeling scared and alone is not “normal discomfort”—it’s distress and neglect.
Boundary setting: She communicated her wishes clearly multiple times; these were disregarded.

Psychologically:

Her repeated statements to her husband about how he ruined the birth reflect trauma processing and attempts to be heard.
She is asserting agency in a situation where she had very little control.
Her strong reaction is a normal response to being disregarded during a major life event.
2. The Husband

His behavior shows controlling and dismissive tendencies.

Ignoring the mother’s repeated, clear wishes about the birth plan.
Involving family and a doula to reinforce his preference, rather than supporting his partner.
Dismissing trauma afterward with platitudes like “mothers are strong” and eye-rolling.

Psychologically:

Displays control over his partner’s body and decisions
Shows lack of empathy and validation
Minimizes real emotional and physical trauma
3. The Doula and Mother-in-Law
Both pushed the home birth preference against the mother’s expressed wishes.
Contributed to a sense of isolation and coercion, which amplified stress during labor.
4. The Context

Childbirth is inherently intense, but the trauma here is preventable:

Labor was extended and unsupported, despite clear communication about risk and comfort.
The mother’s mental health and safety were not prioritized.
Her husband’s dismissive attitude afterward compounds the emotional impact.
⚖️ Verdict

NTA (Not The Asshole)

Reasons:

She repeatedly expressed her wishes and boundaries ✅
Her husband ignored her autonomy and created a stressful, unsafe labor environment ✅
Expressing anger and trauma afterward is a healthy and necessary response ✅

It’s important to note: trauma is valid even if the baby was healthy. Loving her child does not erase the negative birth experience.

Her insistence that any future childbirth respects her boundaries is entirely reasonable.

💬 Final Thoughts

Key takeaways:

Consent and autonomy matter: No one should override your birth plan.
Birth trauma is real: It is normal to feel anger and grief over how the experience was handled.
Validation is critical: Dismissing someone’s experience worsens trauma.

This story highlights that love for a child does not negate the need for respect and support during birth. Being a “strong mother” is not measured by compliance—it’s about being heard, safe, and supported.
